*Quiz (5 questions):*
1. What are the six core principles of visual design? — **Answer:** Balance, contrast, alignment, proximity, repetition, and white space
2. What is visual hierarchy and why is it important? — **Answer:** The arrangement of elements to show their order of importance; it guides users' attention to the most critical information first
3. What is the rule of thirds in composition? — **Answer:** Dividing a canvas into a 3x3 grid and placing key elements along the lines or intersections for balanced, engaging compositions
4. What is the difference between warm and cool colors in design? — **Answer:** Warm colors (red, orange, yellow) evoke energy and urgency; cool colors (blue, green, purple) evoke calmness and trust
5. Why is white space important in UI design? — **Answer:** It reduces cognitive load, improves readability, creates visual breathing room, and helps establish hierarchy

*Quiz (5 questions):*
1. What is the difference between a typeface and a font? — **Answer:** A typeface is the design family (e.g., Inter); a font is a specific weight/size/style within that family (e.g., Inter Bold 16px)
2. What is a type scale? — **Answer:** A systematic progression of font sizes that creates visual harmony and hierarchy (e.g., 12, 14, 16, 20, 24, 32, 48px)
3. How many typefaces should you use in a single design? — **Answer:** Typically 1-2 typefaces; one for headings and one for body text, or a single versatile typeface family
4. What is line height (leading) and why does it matter? — **Answer:** The vertical space between lines of text; proper line height (1.4-1.6x font size for body text) improves readability
5. What makes a typeface accessible? — **Answer:** Clear letterforms, good x-height, distinct characters (e.g., I vs l vs 1), and legibility at small sizes

2. Why is empathy the first stage of design thinking? — **Answer:** Because you can't solve a user's problem without first understanding their needs, context, and pain points

*Quiz (5 questions):*
1. What is the difference between a frame and a group in Figma? — **Answer:** Frames are containers with their own properties (size, constraints, auto layout); groups simply bundle layers together without independent properties
2. What is Auto Layout and why is it useful? — **Answer:** A feature that automatically adjusts spacing and sizing of elements within a frame; it makes designs responsive and easier to maintain
3. What is a component in Figma? — **Answer:** A reusable design element; changes to the master component propagate to all instances
4. What is the difference between a component and a variant? — **Answer:** A component is a reusable element; variants are different states/styles of the same component (e.g., button default, hover, disabled)
5. Why is Figma better than Sketch or Adobe XD for team collaboration? — **Answer:** Figma is browser-based with real-time multi-user editing, built-in commenting, and no file management overhead

1. What is the difference between formative and summative research? — **Answer:** Formative research happens early to understand problems and shape design; summative research happens later to evaluate the final design
2. What is a persona? — **Answer:** A fictional representation of a target user type, based on real research data, that helps designers empathize with and design for specific user needs

5. Why should designers avoid asking users what they want? — **Answer:** Users are bad at designing solutions; instead, ask about their behaviors, pain points, and current workarounds

*Quiz (5 questions):*
1. What is Hick's Law? — **Answer:** The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices; simplify options to speed up decisions
2. What is Fitts's Law? — **Answer:** The time to reach a target depends on its size and distance; make important buttons large and close to where users are
3. What is the Gestalt principle of proximity? — **Answer:** Elements close to each other are perceived as related; use spacing to group related items
4. What is the anchoring bias? — **Answer:** Users rely heavily on the first piece of information they see; use it strategically (e.g., showing the original price before a discount)
5. What is Miller's Law? — **Answer:** The average person can hold 7±2 items in working memory; chunk information into groups of 5-9 for better comprehension

1. What is the difference between a wireframe and a prototype? — **Answer:** A wireframe is a static layout blueprint; a prototype is an interactive simulation that demonstrates user flow
2. When should you use low-fidelity wireframes? — **Answer:** Early in the design process to explore concepts quickly, validate structure, and get feedback before investing in visual design
3. What is prototype fidelity? — **Answer:** The level of detail and interactivity in a prototype, ranging from simple sketches (low-fi) to pixel-perfect interactive simulations (high-fi)
4. What should a wireframe include? — **Answer:** Layout structure, content hierarchy, navigation, and key UI elements — without colors, images, or detailed styling
5. Why prototype before building? — **Answer:** To test usability, validate design decisions, communicate intent to stakeholders, and catch problems before expensive development begins

5. What is the difference between a fluid and a fixed layout? — **Answer:** Fluid layouts adapt to any screen size using percentages; fixed layouts use pixel values and look the same on all screens

3. What is explainable AI in UX? — **Answer:** Designing interfaces that help users understand why an AI made a particular recommendation or decision
4. What is the key difference between designing for screens vs voice? — **Answer:** Voice is linear and ephemeral (no visual hierarchy); users can't scan — everything must be heard sequentially
5. What is the AR design principle of "context awareness"? — **Answer:** AR interfaces must understand and adapt to the physical environment, not overlay digital content blindly
