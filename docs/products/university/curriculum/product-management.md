## YEAR 100 — PM FOUNDATIONS

**Goal:** Produce graduates who can guide products from conception to launch by mastering strategy, execution, user empathy, and data-informed decision-making.

3. In the RICE prioritization framework, what do the letters stand for? — **Answer:** Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort

1. What are the four values of the Agile Manifesto? — **Answer:** Individuals and interactions over processes and tools; Working software over comprehensive documentation; Customer collaboration over contract negotiation; Responding to change over following a plan
2. What are the three Scrum roles? — **Answer:** Product Owner, Scrum Master, Developers

**PM102 — Writing User Stories & Requirements** *(3 Credits)*
*Description:* Learn to write clear, actionable user stories and acceptance criteria that engineers can build from. This course covers the INVEST criteria, story mapping, acceptance criteria formats, and common anti-patterns that cause rework and confusion.

*Classes:*
1. What is a User Story? — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lU9oK5Ln7Rg

5. Common User Story Anti-Patterns — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lU9oK5Ln7Rg

*AI Summary:* User stories are the bridge between product thinking and engineering execution. Good stories are small, testable, and user-focused. Students learn the INVEST criteria, how to write acceptance criteria in Given/When/Then format, and how to avoid common pitfalls like epic-sized stories and solution-focused writing.

*Related Articles:*
- User Stories: Definition and Examples — https://www.atlassian.com/agile/project-management/user-stories
- INVEST in Good User Stories — https://xp123.com/articles/invest-in-good-stories/

*Quiz (5 questions):*
1. What does INVEST stand for in user story writing? — **Answer:** Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable
2. Write a well-formed user story using the standard format. — **Answer:** "As a [type of user], I want [goal] so that [reason/benefit]"
3. What is the difference between a user story and an epic? — **Answer:** An epic is a large body of work that can be broken down into multiple user stories; stories are small enough to complete in one sprint
4. What is Given/When/Then format for acceptance criteria? — **Answer:** Given [context/starting state], When [action taken], Then [expected outcome]
5. Name two common user story anti-patterns. — **Answer:** Stories too large for one sprint, stories written as technical tasks instead of user value, stories with hidden acceptance criteria

*Quiz (5 questions):*
1. What is the difference between a vanity metric and an actionable metric? — **Answer:** Vanity metrics look good but don't drive decisions (e.g., total downloads); actionable metrics inform specific decisions (e.g., activation rate)
2. What is a conversion funnel? — **Answer:** The sequence of steps a user takes to complete a goal, showing where users drop off at each stage
3. What is cohort analysis? — **Answer:** Grouping users by a shared characteristic (e.g., sign-up date) and tracking their behavior over time to identify patterns
4. Name three key metrics a PM should track for a SaaS product. — **Answer:** Activation rate, retention/churn rate, Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)
5. Why is it important to define success metrics before launching a feature? — **Answer:** To objectively measure whether the feature achieved its intended outcome, rather than retroactively justifying the decision

*Description:* Advanced roadmapping techniques, strategy frameworks, and stakeholder alignment. Students learn to build outcome-based roadmaps, connect strategy to execution, and communicate roadmaps to different audiences.

*Classes:*

*AI Summary:* A roadmap is a strategic communication tool, not a Gantt chart. Students learn to build outcome-based roadmaps that focus on problems to solve rather than features to ship, and how to adapt roadmaps as new information emerges.

*Related Articles:*
- Product Roadmaps: A Complete Guide — https://www.productplan.com/learn/what-is-a-product-roadmap/

*Quiz (5 questions):*
1. What is the difference between an output roadmap and an outcome roadmap? — **Answer:** Output lists features to build; outcome lists problems to solve and measurable results to achieve
2. Why should roadmaps be theme-based rather than feature-based? — **Answer:** Themes focus on user problems and business goals, giving teams flexibility in how to solve them
3. How should a PM present a roadmap to engineers vs executives? — **Answer:** Engineers need detail on scope and dependencies; executives need strategic alignment, timelines, and business impact
4. What is a "now-next-later" roadmap? — **Answer:** A roadmap format that groups work into three time horizons: what's being built now, what's coming next, and what's being considered later
5. When should a roadmap be updated? — **Answer:** Continuously, as new data, feedback, and priorities emerge — it's a living document, not a fixed plan

*Quiz (5 questions):*
1. What is a product hypothesis? — **Answer:** A testable statement predicting that a specific change will produce a measurable outcome (e.g., "Adding social login will increase sign-ups by 15%")
2. What is statistical significance and why does it matter? — **Answer:** A measure of confidence that results are not due to random chance; it ensures decisions are based on real effects, not noise
3. What is confirmation bias in product decisions? — **Answer:** The tendency to seek and interpret data that confirms pre-existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence
4. What is the difference between correlation and causation? — **Answer:** Correlation means two variables move together; causation means one variable directly causes the other — correlation does not prove causation
5. When should a PM use qualitative data vs quantitative data? — **Answer:** Qualitative for understanding "why" (user motivations, pain points); quantitative for measuring "what" and "how much" (behavior patterns, scale)

1. What is "The Mom Test" principle in user interviews? — **Answer:** Ask questions about users' past behaviors and current problems, not about your idea — people lie to be nice
2. What is a leading question and why should it be avoided? — **Answer:** A question that suggests a desired answer (e.g., "Don't you think this feature is useful?"); it biases responses and produces unreliable data
3. What is affinity mapping? — **Answer:** A technique for organizing qualitative data by grouping similar observations into themes to identify patterns
4. When should you use surveys vs interviews? — **Answer:** Surveys for quantitative data from many respondents; interviews for deep qualitative understanding from fewer participants
5. What is the ideal number of participants for a usability study? — **Answer:** 5 users typically uncover ~85% of usability problems; diminishing returns after 5-8 participants

4. What is a microservice architecture? — **Answer:** An architecture where an application is built as a collection of small, independent services that communicate via APIs
5. Why should a PM understand technical debt? — **Answer:** To balance feature delivery with code quality, advocate for refactoring time, and understand why engineering sometimes needs to slow down

4. What are the common pricing models for SaaS products? — **Answer:** Freemium, tiered pricing, usage-based pricing, per-seat pricing, flat-rate pricing
5. What is the Business Model Canvas? — **Answer:** A one-page strategic template with 9 blocks covering value proposition, customers, channels, revenue, costs, and key activities
